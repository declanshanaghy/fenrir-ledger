The Home Depot Credit Card Saga
UPDATE: As of December 2024, Home Depot has changed their export format again. This field report documents the third format change in six months. See Issue #461 for the latest parser updates.

It started innocently enough. A user reported that their Home Depot card transactions weren't importing correctly. "Should be a quick fix," I thought. Three days later, I emerged from the parsing trenches with a thousand-yard stare and this field report.

Grievance #1

The Column Headers That Weren't

Home Depot exports CSV files without column headers, but with a 12-line preamble of account information.

Most banks put column headers on line 1. Chase might add a single summary line above. Home Depot? They give you a manifesto. Account number, statement period, available credit, payment due date, minimum payment amount — all before you reach the actual transaction data. And no headers when you get there. Just raw data, columns unnamed, meaning inferred.

What We Want
Date,Description,Amount
2024-12-01,"PURCHASE - LUMBER",129.99
2024-12-02,"PURCHASE - PAINT",45.67
What We Get
Account: ****1234
Statement Period: 11/01 - 11/30
[... 10 more lines ...]
12/01/2024,LUMBER DEPT 0934,129.99,,
12/02/2024,PAINT - BEHR PREMIUM,45.67,,
Grievance #2

Date Formats From The Abyss

Sometimes MM/DD/YYYY. Sometimes M/D/YY. Sometimes "Dec 1, 2024". In the same file.

The date parser started simple. Then it grew. Now it's a 200-line monster that handles seventeen different date formats, including one that appears to be European but only on Tuesdays. I've seen dates with forward slashes, backslashes, dots, and in one memorable case, emoji separators (that was a corrupted export, but still).

The Hunt List: Edge Cases Found

Issue Frequency Impact Solution
Missing column headers 100% of exports Parser fails immediately Pattern matching on line 13+
Inconsistent date formats ~40% of files Transactions on wrong dates Multi-format date parser
Commas in descriptions ~15% of transactions Column misalignment Quoted field detection
Negative amounts as "(123.45)" All returns/credits Credits import as charges Parenthesis parser
Random UTF-16 encoding ~5% of exports Complete gibberish Encoding detection library

The Solution

We built a dedicated Home Depot parser. It's not elegant. It's not clean. But it works. The parser now:

  • Skips the first 12 lines automatically
  • Infers column positions from data patterns
  • Handles all seventeen date format variations
  • Detects and converts parenthetical negatives
  • Auto-detects file encoding

The lesson? Every bank's export is a unique snowflake of chaos. Plan accordingly. Document everything. And keep a bottle of something strong in your desk drawer.
